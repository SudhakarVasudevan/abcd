Biological Mechanism

Why Diabetic Wounds Fail to Heal — and How Surgicoll-Mesh Overcomes This

Most chronic wounds fall into three categories: pressure ulcers, venous ulcers, and diabetic ulcers. Mustoe et al (2006) proposes a unifying hypothesis of chronic wound pathogenesis based on four main causative factors — hypoxia, bacterial infection, repetitive ischaemia-reperfusion injury, and an altered cellular response.

In diabetic patients, excess blood glucose attaches to newly formed collagen fibrils (glycosylation), inhibiting lysyl oxidase-mediated maturation. This produces non-cross-linked, immature collagen that cannot form the structural bridge needed for wound closure — resulting in chronically stalled wounds.

Surgicoll-Mesh collagen is charge-modified (phosphorylated) — dramatically reducing glycosylation of the repair site and enabling normal matured, cross-linked collagen formation. Combined with its non-immunogenic, native fibril architecture, this restores the wound healing cascade even in chronically impaired tissue.

Application Protocol

Surgicoll-Mesh Dressing Protocol

Clinically established protocol for application of Surgicoll-Mesh on chronic diabetic ulcer wounds — no pre-washing required.

01
Wound Assessment

Measure and photograph wound. Document depth, exudate, wound edges and peri-wound condition. Classify wound type and chronicity.

02
Sharp Debridement

Thorough sharp or surgical debridement of necrotic tissue, fibrin slough and non-viable wound bed prior to application.

03
Surgicoll-Mesh Application

No pre-washing required. Trim mesh to wound dimensions. Apply directly to wound bed — can be sutured or stapled in position. Overlapping edges optional.

04
Secondary Dressing

Apply appropriate secondary dressing over mesh — non-adherent, moisture-retaining dressings preferred. Compression bandaging for venous ulcers as indicated.

05
Periodic Monitoring

Monitor wound size and condition at regular intervals. Continue repeat dressings with Surgicoll-Mesh. Expect granulation by Day 4–5. Re-measure wound area weekly.

Biochemical Science

Why It Works in Diabetic Wounds

The three interlocking mechanisms that make Surgicoll-Mesh uniquely effective in diabetic and chronic wound environments.

Anti-Glycosylation Effect

In diabetic patients, blood glucose attaches to newly formed collagen — a process called glycosylation — that inhibits lysyl oxidase-mediated maturation. Surgicoll-Mesh's bioactive phosphorylated collagen dramatically reduces glycosylation of the wound repair zone, enabling normal cross-linked collagen formation and wound closure to proceed.

Mechanism documented in diabetic ulcer biochemistry studies. Healicoll/Surgicoll-Mesh equi clinical data.
Cell-Signal Transduction

High-purity, charge-modified (phosphorylated) Type-I collagen activates specific cell-surface signalling pathways. This attracts fibroblasts, macrophages and endothelial cells to the wound matrix — initiating the healing cascade that is stalled in chronic wounds. Cell infiltration begins within Days 1–3, neo-vascularisation within 4–5 days — verified by histology and electron microscopy.

Stanford University cell-culture experiments documented for charge-modified Type-I collagen.
Non-Immunogenic Scaffold

Unlike tissue-derived wound matrices (Apligraf, OASIS, Alloderm) that contain elastin, Type-III collagen, glycosaminoglycans and other immunogenic molecules, Surgicoll-Mesh is >97% pure Type-I collagen — free of all immunogenic contaminants. No cross-linking is needed — preserving full bioactivity. The 20μ porosity native fibril structure provides optimal cell ingrowth architecture unavailable in lyophilised competitor products.
